Identity and Values

12 January 2023

Values

  • Integrity: This means to cultivate trust in yourself and others, and that your word can be trusted.
  • Intelligence: To value feedback, always try and find the best solution, and encourage critical thinking.
  • Courage: Push yourself to achieve, to strive, and to always explore new things.

Stories

I documented a well-used and complex process for an overseas vendor of our software who was completely out of our timezone. Common issues were only being resolved overnight and causing some friction between them and the people they were supporting. The process involved trust (figuring out what they need and trusting them with the right access), critical thinking (completely putting yourself in their shoes to make sure there were no documentation gaps), using feedback (creating a guide with fixes to the problems they normally encountered) and handover (training their team on how to use the documentation)

Testimonial

The testimonials I got from the above story mainly came from other support staff, who noticed a marked drop in tickets lodged with our helpdesk (roughly 90%+ drop)

Achievements

Including the achievement above, I keep a folder in my inbox for compliments that people give you at work for the work you do. Most of the time people will appreciate what you do but you need to value the times when they put that feedback in writing. Also, I consider personal achievements as well - education gained, family milestones, personal achievements (Drivers Licence, etc)

Where do my values come from?

My values come from my upbringing, my family, my teachers, and myself.

Blog

  1. Ethical dilemma:
    One good example I remember is that a change had been made by a peer that I spotted on a production environment, which caused a sync process to stop running (without the client being notified). I raised it with the person who made the change, and was dissatisfied with the response so took it to my manager - at that time, the requirements to fix the issue were complex (the change in question was an essential bug fix) and so the issue was disregarded. A few months down the track the client noticed and raised a priority incident, and I was able to provide my manager with the full details of what had happened and that they had signed off on it. The outcome of this was that my recommendations were taken into consideration and eventually a Change Manager was employed.
  2. Culture and influences:
    I have a blended family - my parents are of Maori and Dutch descent, and my wife is Japanese - we are very multi-cultural and diverse within our family and friends.
  3. Strengths and learning support:
    I have a lot of experience in IT, problem solving processes, and I have always studied throughout my career. I'm not afraid to take measured risks and take on responsibility, and have been a Project Lead on several occasions. I'm also very strong with documentation and setting up transparent processes for tracking progress on large projects.
  4. Learning and career limitation evaluation:
    My biggest career limitation is that I can work very quickly but make a lot of errors when I do so. As a result, I regularly need to assess myself on my work speed and make sure I don't rush my work, as well as set reasonable expectations and deadlines for the work that I take on, and keep people updated with my progress.
  5. Example with working with others, resistance / tension:
    At one time I was required to set up a new (replacement) computer for the company Office Manager (2IC of the company). She was already unhappy with the failure of her existing computer and required everything on her new computer to be exactly the same as the old one, including software and bookmarks. The pressure was high with this one due to her exacting standards and forward personality, however by working with the CEO of the company (for distraction) I was able to swap out her computer with minimal downtime and fuss.
